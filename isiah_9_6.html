A Linguistic & Historical Analysis

Examining Kingship and Divinity in Isaiah 9:6 [Hebrew 9:5]

The Argument Under Examination

This analysis addresses the traditional Christian polemical argument which posits Isaiah 9:6 as a direct prophecy of Jesus's divinity. The argument is typically constructed as follows:

Premise 1: A Prophecy of Divine Titles

Isaiah 9:6 prophesies that a child will be born and given a series of names, including “Mighty God” (אֵל גִּבּוֹר / El Gibbor) and “Everlasting Father” (אֲבִי־עַד / Avi-Ad). These titles are understood to be literal declarations of the child’s divine nature.

Premise 2: A Fulfillment in Jesus of Nazareth

The New Testament identifies Jesus as the fulfillment of Isaiah’s prophecies (e.g., Matthew 1:23). Therefore, the divine titles prophesied by Isaiah apply directly and ontologically to Jesus.

Conclusion: A Proof of Divinity

Therefore, Isaiah 9:6 is a definitive proof from the Hebrew Bible that the Messiah was prophesied to be God incarnate, a belief fulfilled in the person of Jesus.

The following five-part analysis demonstrates that this syllogism is untenable from linguistic, doctrinal, logical, historical, and textual perspectives, arguing that Isaiah 9:6 describes a human king, likely Hezekiah, in a historical context.

The Active Verb Vayikra: God as the Namer

1. Description

The Hebrew verb וַיִּקְרָא (vayikra, “and he called”) is in the active voice, requiring a subject, typically God in biblical naming contexts. Christian translations render it passively (“he will be called”) to apply divine titles to the child, but this lacks grammatical support. The correct passive form would be יִקָּרֵא (yikkare).

2. Example (The Evidence)

Leviticus 1:1 uses וַיִּקְרָא with God as the implicit subject, establishing a pattern of divine naming:

וַיִּקְרָא אֶל־מֹשֶׁה

vayikra el-Moshe

"And He [God] called to Moses."

3. Application

In Isaiah 9:5, the active verb indicates that God is the one doing the naming. Jewish exegesis interprets this as God—who is Himself described by the titles "Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father"—naming the child "Prince of Peace" (Sar Shalom). The long string of titles describes the Namer, not the one being named.

4. Conclusion

The active verb וַיִּקְרָא unequivocally indicates God names the child, assigning the divine titles to Himself. This grammatical reading, reinforced by early Jewish translations and syntactic norms, dismantles the divine interpretation, which relies on an unsupported change from active to passive voice.

Lexical Meanings: El Gibbor as Metaphorical

1. Description

The term אֵל גִּבּוֹר (El Gibbor) can mean "Mighty God," but אֵל (El) fundamentally means "power" or "might" and is used metaphorically for powerful humans, reflecting heroic strength, not ontological divinity. Pro-deity claims that this term is exclusively divine are overstated, as biblical, lexical, and regional evidence supports its non-divine flexibility.

2. Example (The Evidence)

Ezekiel 32:21 uses a variant of אֵל for human warriors in the underworld:

אֵילֵי גִבּוֹרִים

eilei gibborim

"mighty heroes."

3. Application

In Isaiah 9:5, אֵל גִּבּוֹר is best understood as "mighty hero" or "divine warrior," a title reflecting the king’s God-given strength. This aligns with other uses of divine-like titles for humans, such as Moses (Exodus 7:1) and judges (Psalm 82:1). While Isaiah 10:21 uses the same term for YHWH, this reinforces the interpretation that it is God (the El Gibbor) who is naming the child. Ancient Near Eastern parallels, such as Ugaritic kings called “sons of El” or Mesopotamian kings as “divine” representatives, confirm that divine titles for rulers are honorific, not ontological.

4. Conclusion

אֵל גִּבּוֹר is an honorific title, not a unique divine designation. Its metaphorical use, supported by multiple biblical examples, regional parallels, and early translations, makes the anti-deity interpretation more coherent than a literal divine reading.

Lexical Meanings: Avi-Ad as Royal Hyperbole

1. Description

The title אֲבִי־עַד (Avi-Ad, “Everlasting Father”) is best understood as royal hyperbole for a lasting reign, meaning "father of the age," not a claim of literal divinity. This is especially problematic for Trinitarian theology, which distinguishes the Son from the Father.

2. Example (The Evidence)

In Isaiah 22:21, the human official Eliakim is called a "father" to the nation:

וְהָיָה לְאָב

vehaya le-av

"and he shall be a father."

3. Application

In Isaiah 9:5, אֲבִי־עַד signifies "father of the age," a king whose dynasty will endure. The term עַד (ad) often means "for an age" or a long duration, not absolute eternity. Calling the child "Father" also risks the heresy of modalism, which contradicts orthodox Trinitarian theology.

4. Conclusion

אֲבִי־עַד is a royal title for a human king’s enduring role, not a divine attribute. This reading avoids theological paradoxes and aligns with Hebrew naming conventions.

Radical Monotheism in Isaiah

1. Description

Isaiah’s uncompromising monotheism, emphasizing YHWH as the sole God, precludes a divine Messiah, as a second divine figure would contradict the text’s core theology.

2. Example (The Evidence)

Isaiah 45:5 declares strict monotheism:

אֲנִי יְהוָה וְאֵין עוֹד זוּלָתִי אֵין אֱלֹהִים

ani YHWH ve-ein od zulati ein Elohim

"I am YHWH, and there is no other; besides me there is no God."

3. Application

A divine child in Isaiah 9:5 would imply polytheism, a concept foreign to 8th-century BCE Israel. The titles must be honorific to maintain theological consistency with Isaiah's own message in chapters 40-46.

4. Conclusion

Isaiah’s monotheism makes a divine Messiah incoherent without anachronistic Trinitarian assumptions, supporting a human king with God-given titles.

Theophoric Naming and Royal Ideology

1. Description

Theophoric names in ancient Israel include divine elements (e.g., אֵל / El, יָהוּ / Yahu) to attribute qualities to God, not to deify the bearer, fitting Isaiah 9:6’s royal context.

2. Example (The Evidence)

Hezekiah’s name reflects God’s power, not the king’s divinity:

חִזְקִיָּהוּ

Chizkiyahu

"YHWH is my strength."

3. Application

In Isaiah 9:5, the titles are theophoric, celebrating God’s strength through the king. Ancient Near Eastern parallels, such as Ugaritic kings called “sons of El” or Egyptian kings as “son of Ra,” reinforce this practice.

4. Conclusion

Theophoric naming explains Isaiah 9:6’s titles as honoring God’s work through a human king, fully consistent with Israelite and regional traditions.

Fallacies of Presumption & Context

Anachronism: Imposing Trinitarian Concepts

Description: Anachronism occurs when later theological concepts, like the Trinity, are projected onto an earlier text, misrepresenting its original intent.

Application: Interpreting Isaiah 9:5 as Trinitarian imposes a post-resurrection, Hellenistic doctrine alien to 8th-century BCE Israel. Isaiah’s audience lacked a “God-man” concept.

Conclusion: The Trinitarian reading is anachronistic, relying on later Christian theology, making the human king interpretation more logical.

Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)

Description: Begging the Question occurs when the conclusion is assumed in the premise, creating circular reasoning.

Application: The pro-deity argument assumes Jesus is divine to interpret Isaiah 9:5’s titles as divine, then uses this to prove his divinity, ignoring non-divine readings.

Conclusion: The argument’s circular assumption of Jesus’ divinity invalidates its logical foundation.

Decontextualization of Revelation

Description: Decontextualization occurs when a text is interpreted without its historical and literary context, leading to misinterpretation.

Application: The pro-deity interpretation removes Isaiah 9:5 from its Assyrian crisis context (734–701 BCE), where Hezekiah’s deliverance is central.

Conclusion: Decontextualizing Isaiah 9:6 from its historical setting supports a human king interpretation, as the divine reading lacks contextual grounding.

Fallacies of Ambiguity & Inconsistency

Equivocation on El

Description: Equivocation occurs when a term with multiple meanings is used as if it has one, leading to a flawed conclusion.

Application: The pro-deity argument assumes אֵל in אֵל גִּבּוֹר denotes deity, ignoring its metaphorical use for humans (Ezekiel 32:21, Exodus 7:1, Psalm 45:6).

Conclusion: Equivocating on אֵל’s meaning creates a false divine claim. Its flexible use supports a human king interpretation.

Special Pleading in Translation

Description: Special pleading occurs when a standard is applied inconsistently to support a desired conclusion, such as translating וַיִּקְרָא as a future passive only in Isaiah 9:6.

Application: The verb וַיִּקְרָא is translated as past tense in hundreds of biblical instances but rendered as “will be called” in Isaiah 9:5 to fit a prophetic model, lacking grammatical justification.

Conclusion: The inconsistent translation of וַיִּקְרָא reveals a bias to support a divine Messiah, undermining the pro-deity argument’s integrity.

Fallacies of Relevance

Suppressed Evidence (Cherry Picking)

Description: Suppressed Evidence occurs when relevant contradictory evidence is ignored to favor a biased interpretation.

Application: The pro-deity argument cites Isaiah 10:21 (אֵל גִּבּוֹר for YHWH) but ignores Ezekiel 32:21, Exodus 7:1, and Psalm 45:6 where divine-like titles are used for humans.

Conclusion: Ignoring non-divine uses of these titles undermines the pro-deity argument, strengthening the metaphorical, human king interpretation.

Appeal to Authority (Ad Verecundiam)

Description: Appeal to Authority occurs when an argument relies on an authority’s opinion rather than evidence, especially when the authority is biased or irrelevant to the context.

Application: The pro-deity argument relies on later Christian interpretations while ignoring earlier Jewish readings (LXX, Targum) that assign divine titles to God, not the child.

Conclusion: Relying on biased Christian authorities over earlier, contextually relevant Jewish interpretations weakens the pro-deity argument.

Assyrian Crisis Context (734–701 BCE)

1. Description

Isaiah 7-12’s prophecies address the Syro-Ephraimite and Assyrian crises, making a contemporary king like Hezekiah the primary referent, not a future divine Messiah. The text’s immediate historical focus and hyperbolic language align with celebrating a human king’s role in divine deliverance.

2. Example (The Evidence)

2 Kings 18:5 praises Hezekiah’s piety during Jerusalem’s deliverance from Assyria in 701 BCE:

וְלֹא־הָיָה כָמֹהוּ

velo-haya kamohu

"there was no one like him."

3. Application

Isaiah 9:5 celebrates Hezekiah’s role in the Assyrian defeat. The Talmud (Sanhedrin 94a) suggests Hezekiah as a potential Messiah, reinforcing the immediate context. The prophecy’s urgency fits a contemporary king, not a figure 700 years later. Intertextual links to other messianic passages (Isaiah 11, 42, 53; Zech. 9) describe a human figure empowered by God, not a divine being, consistent with Hezekiah's historical role.

4. Conclusion

The Assyrian crisis context demands a focus on Hezekiah. The eternal reign is poetic hyperbole, and intertextual links describe a human figure empowered by God. This renders the divine Messiah claim irrelevant to Isaiah’s audience.

Context of Isaiah 7, 9, and 11

1. Description

Linking Isaiah 7’s “Emmanuel” to Isaiah 9 and 11’s figures implies multiple divine births, creating theological inconsistency for the pro-deity view.

2. Example (The Evidence)

Isaiah 7:14 refers to a contemporary birth:

הִנֵּה הָעַלְמָה הָרָה וְיֹלֶדֶת בֵּן

hinneh ha-almah hara veyoledet ben

"the young woman is pregnant and bearing a son."

3. Application

Isaiah 7’s עַלְמָה (alma, “young woman”) indicates a present event. Linking this to Isaiah 9 suggests two divine births, problematic for Trinitarian theology. Isaiah 11’s human Messiah, focused on God’s spirit (רוּחַ יְהוָה / ruach YHWH), not divinity, further supports this distinction.

4. Conclusion

The distinct contexts of Isaiah 7 and 9 undermine the pro-deity’s unified divine Messiah, supporting separate human figures, likely Hezekiah.

Septuagint and Targum Interpretations

1. Description

Early Jewish translations (Septuagint, Targum Jonathan) avoid deifying the child, reflecting a non-divine understanding in pre-Christian communities.

2. Example (The Evidence)

The LXX omits divine titles, while the Targum assigns them to God:

Septuagint (LXX): καλεῖται τὸ ὄνομα αὐτοῦ μεγάλης βουλῆς ἄγγελος (kaleitai to onoma autou megales boules angelos, “his name is called Messenger of Great Counsel”).

Targum Jonathan: אִתְקְרִי שְׁמֵיהּ מִן קֳדָם פְּלִי יוֹעֵץ אֱלָהָא גִבָּרָא (itkri shemeih min kodam peli yoetz elaha gibbara, “his name was called from the presence of Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God”).

3. Application

The LXX and Targum’s non-divine renderings, supported by textual variants and Jewish tradition (Dead Sea Scrolls, 4QFlorilegium), confirm a human king interpretation.

4. Conclusion

The ancient translations prove that the divine interpretation is a later, revisionist reading, contrary to the established tradition.

New Testament Silence and Typological Usage

1. Description

The absence of Isaiah 9:6 citations in the New Testament, despite its supposed centrality to Jesus’ divinity, and the typological use of related texts, question its divine significance.

2. Example (The Evidence)

Matthew 4:15-16 cites Isaiah 9:1-2 but conspicuously omits 9:6.

3. Application

Despite its apparent value, Isaiah 9:6 is never quoted in the NT, unlike Isaiah 7:14 (Matthew 1:23) or Isaiah 53. The use of related texts is typological, not direct proof of divinity. If 9:6 were foundational, its absence is significant.

4. Conclusion

The NT’s silence on Isaiah 9:6 weakens its pro-deity significance, supporting a non-divine interpretation consistent with early Jewish readings.

1. Messianic Context and Intertextual Links

Pro-Deity Argument (Christian Position)

Isaiah 9:6 is a messianic prophecy, as evidenced by intertextual connections with Isaiah 11:1-5, 10 (a shoot from Jesse), Isaiah 42, 49, 53 (the servant), and Zechariah 9:9-12 (a humble king). These texts describe a figure reigning on David’s throne with eternal justice, a light to the Gentiles, and a suffering servant, fulfilled in Jesus as the divine Messiah. The Targum Jonathan identifies the child as “Messiah,” reinforcing a divine interpretation.

Anti-Deity Counterargument

Isaiah 9:6 refers to Hezekiah in the Assyrian crisis context (734–701 BCE), supported by Isaiah 36-39 and Talmudic views (Sanhedrin 94a). Intertextual links (Isaiah 11, 42, 49, 53; Zechariah 9:9-12) describe a human figure empowered by God’s spirit (e.g., Isaiah 11:2, רוּחַ יְהוָה / ruach YHWH; Zechariah 9:9, עָנִי / ani, “humble”), not divine. The Targum’s “Messiah” reflects a human deliverer, as Second Temple Judaism (e.g., Dead Sea Scrolls, 1QS 9:11) avoids divine messiahs. Poetic hyperbole in Isaiah 9:7 (e.g., Psalm 72:8, מִיָּם עַד־יָם / miyam ad-yam) fits Hezekiah’s temporary peace.

Winner & Reasoning

Anti-Deity: The anti-deity position prevails due to its robust historical and textual analysis. The Assyrian crisis context and Hezekiah’s role (2 Kings 18:5; Isaiah 36-39) align better with Isaiah 9:6-7’s immediate focus than a distant divine Messiah. Intertextual links emphasize human attributes (e.g., Isaiah 42:1, נָתַתִּי רוּחִי / natatti ruchiy), and Second Temple evidence (Talmud, Dead Sea Scrolls) supports a human messianic figure. The pro-deity links are valid but assume a divine ontology not explicit in the texts, and the Targum’s human messianic focus undermines divinity claims.

2. Divine Titles Imply Deity

Pro-Deity Argument (Christian Position)

The titles “Mighty God” (אֵל גִּבּוֹר / El Gibbor) and “Everlasting Father” (אֲבִי־עַד / Avi-Ad) denote divinity. “El Gibbor” refers to YHWH in Isaiah 10:21, and “El” (אֵל) denotes deity in 217 biblical instances. “Everlasting Father” implies the child as creator of eternity, a divine attribute. The title “Wonderful Counselor” echoes divine wisdom (Isaiah 28:29), and “El” is a noun (deity), not an adjective (e.g., “mighty warrior”).

Anti-Deity Counterargument

“El Gibbor” is metaphorical, as seen in Ezekiel 32:21 (אֵילֵי גִבּוֹרִים / eilei gibborim, “mighty heroes” for humans), Exodus 7:1 (אֱלֹהִים / Elohim for Moses), and Psalm 45:6 (אֱלֹהִים / Elohim for a king). “Avi-Ad” (Father of the Age) is royal hyperbole for a lasting reign, as in Isaiah 22:21 (וְהָיָה לְאָב / vehaya le-av, “a father”). Ancient Near Eastern parallels (e.g., Ugaritic “sons of El”) and the Septuagint’s non-divine rendering (μεγάλης βουλῆς ἄγγελος / megales boules angelos) confirm this. The claim that “El” is exclusively divine ignores non-divine uses (e.g., Job 41:25, אֵילִים / eilim; Psalm 29:1, בְּנֵי אֵלִים / benei elim).

Winner & Reasoning

Anti-Deity: The anti-deity position prevails by providing extensive biblical and cultural evidence for non-divine uses of אֵל (El) and אֲבִי־עַד (Avi-Ad). Ezekiel 32:21 and other examples (e.g., Psalm 82:1, אֱלֹהִים / Elohim for judges) demonstrate “El Gibbor” as metaphorical, and “Avi-Ad” aligns with human leadership (Isaiah 22:21). The Septuagint’s omission of divine titles and Ugaritic parallels strengthen this. The pro-deity reliance on Isaiah 10:21 and “El”’s noun status is valid but ignores contextual flexibility, as the anti-deity’s broader lexical analysis prevails.

3. Prophetic Perfect Supports Future Messiah

Pro-Deity Argument (Christian Position)

The future tense in Christian translations (e.g., NASB, “he will be called”) reflects a prophetic perfect, where future events are described as completed (e.g., Isaiah 5:13, הָלְכוּ / halkhu; Isaiah 53:5, וְהוּא מְחֹלָל / vehu mecholal). This supports a future divine Messiah, fulfilled in Jesus, not a contemporary figure like Hezekiah.

Anti-Deity Counterargument

The verb וַיִּקְרָא (vayikra, “and he called”) is active, requiring God as the subject, not a passive future naming. Prophetic perfects describe events (e.g., Isaiah 5:13, exile; Isaiah 53:5, suffering), not naming acts, which use active verbs (e.g., Genesis 17:5, וַיִּקְרָא / vayikra; Judges 13:24, וַתִּקְרָא / vattikra). The Assyrian context (Isaiah 9:4, שְׁבַתָּה / shavatta) ties the naming to an immediate event, likely Hezekiah’s role, not a distant prophecy. The passive יִקָּרֵא (yikkare) is absent, undermining the future passive claim.

Winner & Reasoning

Anti-Deity: The anti-deity position prevails by demonstrating that prophetic perfects do not apply to naming acts, which consistently use active verbs with God as the subject (e.g., Genesis 21:3). The absence of a passive verb (יִקָּרֵא / yikkare) and the Assyrian context (Isaiah 9:1-7) align with an immediate naming event, not a future prophecy. The pro-deity examples (Isaiah 5:13, 53:5) are irrelevant to naming, and the anti-deity syntactic analysis is more precise.

4. Hezekiah’s Reign Fails to Fulfill Isaiah 9:7

Pro-Deity Argument (Christian Position)

Hezekiah cannot be the child, as his reign was limited, and his son Manasseh’s idolatry (2 Kings 21:1-6) contradicts Isaiah 9:7’s promise of an eternal, righteous government (וּלְמַרְבֵּה הַמִּשְׂרָה וּלְשָׁלוֹם אֵין־קֵץ / ulemarbeh hammisrah uleshalom ein-ketz, “of the increase of his government and of peace there will be no end”). Only a divine Messiah (Jesus) fits this eternal scope.

Anti-Deity Counterargument

Isaiah 9:7’s “no end” is poetic hyperbole, common in royal poetry (e.g., Psalm 72:7, עַד־בְּלִי יָרֵחַ / ad-beli yareach; Ecclesiastes 4:16, אֵין קֵץ / ein ketz). Hezekiah’s deliverance (701 BCE, Isaiah 36-39) and piety (2 Kings 18:5) fulfill the prophecy’s immediate hope, with “eternal” language idealizing Davidic continuity, not literal eternity. The exile under Manasseh’s successors reflects historical realities, not a failure of the prophecy, as it aimed to inspire hope in crisis.

Winner & Reasoning

Anti-Deity: The anti-deity position prevails by showing that “no end” is hyperbolic, as evidenced by parallels in Psalm 72:7 and Ecclesiastes 4:16, fitting Hezekiah’s temporary peace. The Assyrian context and Talmudic view (Sanhedrin 94a) support Hezekiah as the referent. The pro-deity insistence on literal eternity assumes an eschatological scope not required by the text’s poetic genre, making the anti-deity interpretation more contextually grounded.

5. Trinitarian Implications of Titles

Pro-Deity Argument (Christian Position)

The titles “Mighty God” (אֵל גִּבּוֹר / El Gibbor) and “Everlasting Father” (אֲבִי־עַד / Avi-Ad) support the Trinity, aligning with Jesus as God incarnate. Additional texts like Zechariah 12:10 (“they will look on me, the one they have pierced”) and Malachi 3:1 (“the Lord you are seeking will come to his temple”) reinforce a divine Messiah, fulfilled in Jesus.

Anti-Deity Counterargument

“Mighty God” (אֵל גִּבּוֹר / El Gibbor) and “Everlasting Father” (אֲבִי־עַד / Avi-Ad) are metaphorical, as seen in Jeremiah 33:15 (יְהוָה צִדְקֵנוּ / YHWH tzidkenu, “The Lord is our Righteousness” for Jerusalem), Exodus 7:1 (אֱלֹהִים / Elohim for Moses), and Psalm 45:6 (אֱלֹהִים / Elohim for a king). “Avi-Ad” as “Father of the Age” denotes a human king’s lasting reign, and Trinitarian readings impose anachronistic theology alien to Isaiah’s monotheism (Isaiah 45:5, זוּלָתִי אֵין אֱלֹהִים / zulati ein Elohim). Zechariah 12:10 and Malachi 3:1 describe human figures or God’s actions, not a divine Messiah.

Winner & Reasoning

Anti-Deity: The anti-deity position prevails by demonstrating that divine titles are metaphorical in biblical usage (e.g., Jeremiah 33:15; Psalm 82:1), and “Avi-Ad” avoids Trinitarian modalism. Isaiah’s monotheism (Isaiah 44:6) precludes a divine Messiah, and Zechariah 12:10’s textual variants and Malachi 3:1’s human messenger (Eliyahu, Malachi 4:5) undermine divine claims. The Trinitarian argument is anachronistic, relying on later theology.

6. Targum’s Messianic Reference Supports Deity

Pro-Deity Argument (Christian Position)

The Targum Jonathan identifies the child as “Messiah” (אִתְקְרִי שְׁמֵיהּ מִן קֳדָם / itkri shemeih min kodam), supporting a divine Messiah, as the titles “Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God” align with Jesus’ divinity in Christian theology.

Anti-Deity Counterargument

The Targum assigns divine titles to God, not the child, naming him “Messiah” as a human deliverer (אִתְקְרִי שְׁמֵיהּ מִן קֳדָם פְּלִי יוֹעֵץ אֱלָהָא גִבָּרָא / itkri shemeih min kodam peli yoetz elaha gibbara). Second Temple Jewish messianism (e.g., Dead Sea Scrolls, 1QS 9:11; Psalms of Solomon 17) describes human figures, and the Talmud (Sanhedrin 94a) views Hezekiah as a messianic figure without divinity. The phrase “from the presence of” (מִן קֳדָם / min kodam) separates the titles from the child, aligning with the Masoretic Text’s active verb (וַיִּקְרָא / vayikra).

Winner & Reasoning

Anti-Deity: The anti-deity position prevails by clarifying the Targum’s intent, supported by Second Temple evidence and the Talmud, which view the Messiah as human. The phrase “from the presence of” explicitly assigns divine titles to God, not the child, aligning with the Masoretic Text and Jewish exegesis (Rashi, Commentary on Isaiah 9:6). The pro-deity assumption of divinity misreads the Targum’s human focus.

Final Verdict

The child of Isaiah 9:6 is a human king, likely Hezekiah, celebrated in the context of the Syro-Ephraimite and Assyrian crises (734–701 BCE). His titles—אֵל גִּבּוֹר (El Gibbor, “Mighty Hero”) and אֲבִי־עַד (Avi-Ad, “Father of the Age”)—are theophoric and hyperbolic, reflecting God’s strength and the king’s enduring role, not divinity. The active verb וַיִּקְרָא (vayikra) assigns divine titles to God, not the child. Isaiah’s monotheism, early Jewish translations (LXX, Targum), New Testament silence, poetic conventions (e.g., Psalm 45:6, כִּסְאֲךָ אֱלֹהִים / kiseakha Elohim), and logical fallacies (anachronism, equivocation, special pleading, begging the question, suppressed evidence, appeal to authority, decontextualization) confirm this. The Christian divine interpretation relies on anachronistic Trinitarian assumptions, inconsistent translations, and selective evidence, making the human king interpretation more coherent with the Hebrew text, historical context, and Israelite theology.
