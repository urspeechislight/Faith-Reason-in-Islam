A Theological & Linguistic Analysis

Examining Divine Selection and Sonship in Qur'an 3:42 and 39:4

The Argument Under Examination

Before proceeding to the analysis, it is necessary to faithfully define the polemical argument this study addresses. The argument attempts to prove the divine sonship of Jesus by linking two Qur'anic verses in a logical syllogism, constructed as follows:

Premise 1: A Conditional Blueprint

Qur'an 39:4 ("Had Allah willed to take a son, He would have chosen...") establishes a hypothetical condition for how God *could* take a son: by choosing (`iṣṭifā`) from His creation.

Premise 2: A Factual Fulfillment

Qur'an 3:42 ("Indeed, Allah has chosen you...") states as a fact that God *did* choose (`iṣṭifā`) Maryam.

Conclusion: A Divine Son

Therefore, the argument concludes, Maryam's selection was the fulfillment of the condition for sonship, and Jesus is that chosen, divine son.

The following four-part analysis will demonstrate that this syllogism is untenable from a linguistic, doctrinal, logical, and historical perspective.

The argument conflates distinct Arabic concepts. Classical linguistics and the Qur'an's own vocabulary demonstrate that "choosing a servant" and "taking a son" are separate ideas. This section explores the direct evidence for this distinction, focusing on the key vocabulary.

The Function of `Law` (لَوْ): The Particle of Impossibility

Introduction

In Arabic grammar, the particle `لَوْ` (law) is not a simple conditional "if." Instead, it introduces a counterfactual statement, indicating a scenario that is impossible or contrary to reality. Its primary function is to establish a premise for the sake of argument, often to highlight the absurdity or impossibility of that premise.

Example

Classical Arabic lexicography and poetry consistently use `law` to express an impossible wish or a lament over something that cannot be. For instance, the renowned lexicon `Lisān al-`Arab` defines its function clearly:

لَوْ: حَرْفٌ يَقْتَضِي امْتِناعَ ما يَلِيهِ واسْتِلْزامَهُ لِتالِيهِ

Law: ḥarf yāqtaḍī imtināʿa mā yalīhi wa-istilzāmahu li-tālīhi

"`Law`: A particle that necessitates the impossibility of what follows it, and its necessary connection to what comes after."

- Lisān al-`Arab

Similarly, the pre-Islamic poet Imru' al-Qais uses `law` in his Mu`allaqa to express a wish contrary to his painful reality:

أَلا رُبَّ يَومٍ لَكَ مِنهُنَّ صالِحٌ ... وَلا سِيَّما يَومٍ بِدارَةِ جُلجُلِ

ʾalā rubba yawmin laka minhunna ṣāliḥun ... wa-lā sīyamā yawmin bi-dārati juljuli

فَلَوْ أَنَّهَا نَفْسٌ تَمُوتُ سَوِيَّةً ... وَلَكِنَّهَا نَفْسٌ تَسَاقَطُ أَنْفُسَا

fa-law ʾannahā nafsun tamūtu sawiyyatan ... wa-lakinnahā nafsun tasaqaṭu ʾanfusā

"Many a fine day have I had with them, especially the day at Darat Juljul...

If only it were one soul that died at once, but it is a soul from which other souls fall away."

- Mu`allaqa of Imru' al-Qais
Application

In Qur'an 39:4, the verse states: لَوْ أَرَادَ اللَّهُ أَنْ يَتَّخِذَ وَلَدًا لَاصْطَفَىٰ مِمَّا يَخْلُقُ مَا يَشَاءُ سُبْحَانَهُ

Law arāda Allāhu an yattakhidha waladan laṣṭafā mimmā yakhluqu mā yashāʾu subḥānahu

"Had Allah willed to take a son, He could have chosen from what He creates whatever He wills. Glory be to Him!"

The use of `law` here presents the premise "Allah willed to take a son" as an impossible, counterfactual scenario. The verse's function is not to suggest a real possibility, but rather to construct a hypothetical argument to demonstrate the absurdity and impossibility of God having a son, even if such an idea were to be entertained. The subsequent phrase "Glory be to Him!" (`subḥānahu`) immediately dismisses the entire hypothetical, affirming God's transcendence.

Conclusion

Therefore, understanding the precise grammatical function of `law` is crucial. It signifies negation and impossibility, establishing a premise contrary to divine reality. To interpret Qur'an 39:4 as a blueprint for how God *could* take a son is a fundamental linguistic misinterpretation that reverses the verse's intended meaning of absolute denial.

Lexical Meanings: `Iṣṭifā'` vs. `Ittikhādh`

Iṣṭifā' (اِصْطِفَاء)

Introduction

Root: `ص-ف-و` (Ṣ-F-W - Purity)

Concept: To Choose / To Select the Purest

The term `Iṣṭifā'` (اِصْطِفَاء) denotes an act of divine selection or choice, often implying the purification or elevation of the chosen individual. It signifies that God honors a creature based on its inherent qualities or for a specific, noble task. The one chosen (`muṣṭafā`) remains fundamentally a servant of God, **elevated in status but not changed in essence**.

Example

الصَّفْوُ: خلوص الشيء من الكدر... واصطفاه: اختاره.

Al-Ṣafw: khulūṣu ash-shayʾi min al-kadar... wa-iṣṭafāhu: ikhtārahū.

"Al-Ṣafw: The freedom of a thing from any impurity... and `iṣṭafāhu` means he chose him."

- Lisān al-`Arab

This definition highlights the core meaning of `iṣṭifā'` as choosing something pure or making something pure through selection.

Application

In Qur'an 3:42, Allah's choosing (`iṣṭifā'`) of Maryam is an act of honoring her purity and selecting her for the miraculous birth of a prophet. The verse states: إِنَّ اللَّهَ اصْطَفَاكِ وَطَهَّرَكِ وَاصْطَفَاكِ عَلَىٰ نِسَاءِ الْعَالَمِينَ

Inna Allāha iṣṭafāki wa ṭahharaki wa iṣṭafāki ʿalā nisāʾi al-ʿālamīn

"Indeed, Allah has chosen you and purified you and chosen you above the women of the worlds."

She remains a chosen servant, **elevated in rank but not changed in her created essence**.

Conclusion

`Iṣṭifā'` denotes selection and honor within the realm of creation. It **never implies a change in nature or a share in divinity**.

Ittikhādh (اِتِّخَاذ)

Introduction

Root: `أ-خ-ذ` (ʾ-KH-DH - To Take)

Concept: To Take / To Adopt / To Designate

In contrast, `Ittikhādh` (اِتِّخَاذ) implies an act of taking something for oneself, adopting, or designating it to fulfill a need or desire. When applied to God, as in "taking a son," it suggests a deficiency or a need for companionship, succession, or physical procreation, which is utterly incompatible with God's absolute self-sufficiency and transcendence (`Al-Ghanī`).

Example

واتَّخَذْتُه وليّاً أَي صَيَّرْتُه. وقوله تعالى: واتَّخَذَ الله إِبراهيمَ خَلِيلاً؛ أَي صَيَّرَه خَلِيلاً

Wa-ittakhadhtuhu waliyyan ʾay ṣayyartuhu. Wa-qawluhu taʿālā: Wa-ittakhadha Allāhu Ibrāhīma khalīlan; ʾay ṣayyara-hu khalīlan.

"And `ittakhadhtuhu waliyyan` means I made him a friend/ally. And the statement of the Exalted: `And Allah took (`ittakhadha`) Ibrāhīm as an intimate friend (`khalīl`)`; meaning, He made him an intimate friend."

- Lisān al-`Arab

This illustrates that `ittikhādh` signifies making something into a particular state or for a particular purpose, implying an act of designation or transformation, rather than a mere selection from existing options.

Application

The Qur'an uses `ittikhādh walad` specifically for the prohibited act of "taking a son," a concept rejected as `shirk` (polytheism) because it ascribes need and partnership to God. For example, in Qur'an 39:4: لَوْ أَرَادَ اللَّهُ أَنْ يَتَّخِذَ وَلَدًا

Law arāda Allāhu an yattakhidha waladan

"Had Allah willed to take a son..."

This phrasing implies an act of adoption or designation that is impossible for the Self-Sufficient God.

Conclusion

`Ittikhādh` implies adoption and need, attributes impossible for God. The polemical argument falsely equates this with `iṣṭifā'`, a word with a completely different meaning.

Overall Conclusion of Lexical Meanings

The polemical argument attempts to equate `iṣṭifā'` (as in Qur'an 3:42, "Indeed, Allah has chosen you," referring to Maryam) with `ittikhādh` (as in Qur'an 39:4, "Had Allah willed to take a son"). However, these are distinct linguistic concepts. `Iṣṭifā'` describes God's act of honoring and selecting Maryam for her purity and a miraculous task (the virgin birth of Jesus), without implying any change in her created status. Conversely, `ittikhādh` in the context of sonship implies a relationship of need or procreation, which is vehemently denied for God in the Qur'an. The Qur'an uses `ittikhādh` precisely to refute the notion of divine sonship, as it implies a deficiency in God's absolute self-sufficiency. Conflating `iṣṭifā'` and `ittikhādh` is a lexical fallacy. `Iṣṭifā'` elevates a creature, while `ittikhādh` in the context of sonship describes an act that is impossible for the Creator. The Qur'an's precise choice of words maintains the **absolute transcendence of God and the created nature of all beings**, including prophets like Jesus and his mother Maryam.

2. The Doctrinal Proof

Shi'i exegesis (`tafsīr`) is built upon the teachings of the Ahl al-Bayt (a). Their narrations clarify the explicit purpose of these verses and preemptively refute objections by explaining the true status of Jesus (`Īsā) and his mother Maryam. A core principle is that divine selection (`iṣṭifā`) elevates a creature to a special role, but **never implies a change in its created essence or a share in divinity**.

Principle 1: Qur'an 39:4 is a Verse of Invalidation (`Ibṭāl`)

Introduction

From a Shi'i doctrinal perspective, informed by the narrations of the Ahl al-Bayt (a), Qur'an 39:4 is understood not as a conditional statement outlining a possibility for God to take a son, but rather as a verse of absolute invalidation and rebuttal against such a notion. Its purpose is to definitively deny the concept of divine sonship.

Example

The authoritative Shi'i commentary, `Tafsīr al-Qummī`, explicitly states the context and purpose of Qur'an 39:4:

هو ما قالت النصارى ان المسيح ابن الله... فقال الله عز وجل إبطالا له... وجواب هؤلاء الذين زعموا ذلك في سورة الزمر في قوله : لو أراد الله ان يتخذ ولدا لاصطفى مما يخلق ما يشاء سبحانه.

Huwa mā qālat an-naṣārā anna al-Masīḥa ibnu Allāh... fa-qāla Allāhu ʿazza wa-jalla ibṭālan lahu... wa-jawābu hāʾulāʾi alladhīna zaʿamū dhālika fī Sūrat az-Zumar fī qawlihi: Law arāda Allāhu an yattakhidha waladan laṣṭafā mimmā yakhluqu mā yashāʾu subḥānahu.

"This is what the Christians said, that the Messiah is the son of God... So Allah, the Mighty and Majestic, said, in **invalidation (`ibṭālan`)** of it... And the **answer (`jawāb`)** to those who made this claim is in Sūrat al-Zumar, in His statement: **'Had Allah willed to take a son, He could have chosen from what He creates whatever He wills. Glory be to Him!'**"

- Tafsīr al-Qummī, Vol. 2, p. 69 (from Tafsīr al-Ṣāfī, Vol. 3, p. 335)

This understanding is further supported by `Al-Burhān fī Tafsīr al-Qur’ān`, which quotes the same narration from Ali ibn Ibrahim (Qummi).

Application

This narration from `Tafsīr al-Qummī` is critical because it provides the divinely intended context for Qur'an 39:4. It explicitly labels the verse as an "invalidation" (`ibṭālan`) and an "answer" (`jawāb`) to the Christian claim of divine sonship. This directly contradicts any interpretation that views the verse as a conditional possibility. The verse, therefore, serves as a theological barrier against the very concept it is being used to support by the polemicist.

Conclusion

The clear doctrinal understanding, as transmitted through the Imams (a) via their exegetes, is that Qur'an 39:4 functions as a definitive rejection of divine sonship. To employ it as evidence for such a claim is to fundamentally misunderstand and reverse its revealed purpose, turning a verse of negation into one of affirmation.

Principle 2: The Purpose of the Virgin Birth is to be a Sign (`Āyah`)

Introduction

The virgin birth of Jesus is a central miracle in Islamic belief, affirming Maryam's purity and God's absolute power. However, it is often misinterpreted to imply divine sonship. The Qur'an itself clarifies the precise purpose of this miraculous birth, framing it within the context of God's creative will and as a sign for humanity.

Example

The Qur'an explicitly states the reason for Jesus's unique birth:

وَلِنَجْعَلَهُ آيَةً لِلنَّاسِ وَرَحْمَةً مِنَّا

Wa li-najʿalahu āyatan li-n-nāsi wa raḥmatan minnā

"...And that We may make him a sign (`āyah`) for the people and a mercy from Us."

- Qur'an 19:21

To further emphasize that this miraculous birth does not imply divinity, the Qur'an draws a direct parallel between Jesus's creation and that of Adam:

إِنَّ مَثَلَ عِيسَىٰ عِندَ اللَّهِ كَمَثَلِ آدَمَ ۖ خَلَقَهُ مِن تُرَابٍ ثُمَّ قَالَ لَهُ كُن فَيَكُونُ

Inna mathala ʿĪsā ʿinda Allāhi ka-mathali Ādama ۖ khalaqahu min turābin thumma qāla lahu kun fa-yakūnu.

"Indeed, the example of Jesus to Allah is like that of Adam. He created him from dust; then He said to him, 'Be,' and he was."

- Qur'an 3:59
Application

These verses clearly articulate that Jesus's miraculous birth was intended as a "sign" (`āyah`) of God's unlimited power and mercy, **not as a means to produce a divine son**. The comparison to Adam is particularly potent: if being created without a father made Jesus divine, then Adam, created without both a father and a mother, would have an even greater claim to divinity, a conclusion universally rejected by monotheists. This comparison explicitly refutes any attempt to infer divinity from Jesus's unique mode of birth.

Conclusion

The virgin birth of Jesus is a profound miracle demonstrating God's creative omnipotence, placing Jesus firmly within the lineage of prophets and servants of God. It is a testament to divine power and a sign for humanity, **not an indication of divine sonship or a compromise of God's absolute oneness**.

Principle 3: The Status of `ʿIbād Mukramūn` (Honored Servants) and Conditional Intercession

Introduction

The Qur'an clarifies the true status of those mistakenly called "sons" or "partners" of God, such as angels, Jesus, or other revered figures. It designates them as `ʿibād mukramūn` (honored servants), emphasizing their **created nature and their absolute subservience to God**. Furthermore, it clarifies that their ability to intercede (`shafāʿah`) is not an inherent right but a conditional privilege granted by God, dependent on His approval and the spiritual state of the one being interceded for.

Example

Qur'an 21:26-28 directly addresses the claims of divine offspring:

وَقَالُوا اتَّخَذَ الرَّحْمَٰنُ وَلَدًا ۗ سُبْحَانَهُ ۚ بَلْ عِبَادٌ مُّكْرَمُونَ ۝ لَا يَسْبِقُونَهُ بِالْقَوْلِ وَهُم بِأَمْرِهِ يَعْمَلُونَ ۝ يَعْلَمُ مَا بَيْنَ أَيْدِيهِمْ وَمَا خَلْفَهُمْ وَلَا يَشْفَعُونَ إِلَّا لِمَنِ ارْتَضَىٰ وَهُم مِّنْ خَشْيَتِهِ مُشْفِقُونَ

Wa qālū ittakhadha al-Raḥmānu waladan ۗ subḥānahu ۚ bal ʿibādun mukramūn ۝ lā yasbiqūnahu bil-qawli wa hum bi-amrihi yaʿmalūn ۝ yaʿlamu mā bayna aydīhim wa mā khalfahum wa lā yashfaʿūna illā li-man irtaḍā wa hum min khashyatihi mushfiqūn

"And they say, 'The Most Merciful has taken a son.' Exalted is He! Rather, they are [but] honored servants. They do not precede Him in speech, and they act [only] by His command. He knows what is [present] before them and what will be after them, and they do not intercede except for one whom He approves, and they are from fear of Him apprehensive."

- Qur'an 21:26-28

`Tafsīr al-Qummī` clarifies the target of this verse:

هو ما قالت النصارى إن المسيح ابن الله وما قالت اليهود عزير ابن الله وقالوا في الأئمة عليهم السلام ما قالوا فقال الله سبحانه سبحانه أنفة له بل عباد مكرمون يعني هؤلاء الذين زعموا أنهم ولد الله

"This is what the Christians said, that the Messiah is the son of God, and what the Jews said, that Uzayr is the son of God, and what they said about the Imams (a) what they said. So Allah, glory be to Him, said, in disdain for it: 'Rather, they are honored servants,' meaning those who claimed they were the children of God."

- Tafsīr al-Qummī (from Tafsīr al-Ṣāfī, Vol. 3, p. 335)

Regarding intercession, Imam al-Kazim (a) (via Imam al-Riḍā (a)) clarifies the condition for `shafāʿah`:

قال إنما شفاعتي لأهل الكباير من أمتي فأما المحسنون منهم فما عليهم من سبيل قيل يا بن رسول الله صلى الله عليه وآله كيف يكون الشفاعة لأهل الكبائر والله تعالى يقول ولا يشفعون إلا لمن ارتضى ومن يرتكب الكبيرة لا يكون مرتضى فقال ما من مؤمن يرتكب ذنبا إلا سائه ذلك وندم عليه . وقال النبي صلى الله عليه وآله كفى بالندم توبة وقال من سرته حسنة وساءته سيئة فهو مؤمن فمن لم يندم على ذنب يرتكبه فليس بمؤمن ولم تجب له الشفاعة وكان ظالما والله تعالى ذكره يقول ما للظالمين من حميم ولا شفيع يطاع فقيل له يا ابن رسول الله وكيف لا يكون مؤمنا من لم يندم على ذنب يرتكبه فقال ما من أحد يرتكب كبيرة من المعاصي وهو يعلم أن سيعاقب عليها إلا ندم على ما ارتكب ومتى ندم كان تائبا مستحقا للشفاعة ومتى لم يندم عليها كان مصرا والمصر لا يغفر له لأنه غير مؤمن بعقوبة ما ارتكب ولو كان مؤمنا بالعقوبة لندم وقد قال النبي صلى الله عليه

"He (the Prophet) said: 'Indeed, my intercession is for the perpetrators of major sins among my nation. As for the good-doers among them, there is no way against them.' It was said: 'O son of the Messenger of Allah, how can intercession be for the perpetrators of major sins when Allah, the Exalted, says: "And they do not intercede except for one whom He approves," and one who commits a major sin is not approved?' He said: 'There is no believer who commits a sin except that he feels bad about it and regrets it. And the Prophet (s) said: "Regret is sufficient repentance." And he said: "Whoever is pleased by his good deed and displeased by his bad deed, he is a believer." So whoever does not regret a sin he commits is not a believer, and intercession is not obligatory for him, and he is an oppressor. And Allah, Exalted is His remembrance, says: "For the wrongdoers there will be no devoted friend and no intercessor [who will be] obeyed." It was said to him: 'O son of the Messenger of Allah, how can one who does not regret a sin he commits not be a believer?' He said: 'No one commits a major sin while knowing that he will be punished for it except that he regrets what he committed. And whenever he regrets, he is repentant and worthy of intercession. And whenever he does not regret it, he is persistent [in sin], and the persistent one is not forgiven because he is not a believer in the punishment for what he committed. If he were a believer in the punishment, he would regret it. And the Prophet (s) said: "No major sin with seeking forgiveness, and no minor sin with persistence."'"

- Tafsīr al-Ṣāfī, Vol. 3, p. 336 (from Al-Tawḥīd and Al-Khiṣāl)
Application

These texts collectively refute the idea of inherent divine sonship or unconditional intercession based on ontological status. The Qur'an explicitly corrects the misconception of divine offspring by stating that these figures are merely "honored servants" (`ʿibād mukramūn`). Their actions are entirely by God's command, and their knowledge is limited. Crucially, their ability to intercede is not a right but a divinely granted privilege, contingent upon God's approval of the interceded-for's `dīn` (religion/faith), which includes repentance for sins. This demonstrates that even the highest of creation **remain servants**, subject to God's will and the conditions He sets, **fundamentally incompatible with any notion of divine partnership or sonship**.

Conclusion

The true status of figures like angels and prophets, including Jesus, is one of elevated servitude to God. Their honor and ability to intercede are divine gifts, **not inherent attributes of divinity or sonship**. This doctrinal clarity reinforces the absolute Oneness of God (`Tawḥīd`) and the **created nature of all beings**, firmly refuting any claims that compromise God's unique and indivisible nature.

Principle 4: The Absolute Denial of `Ittikhādh Walad` (Taking a Son) and God's Self-Sufficiency

Introduction

The concept of God "taking a son" (`ittikhādh walad`) is fundamentally rejected in Islamic doctrine, as it implies a deficiency, need, or partnership that is incompatible with God's absolute self-sufficiency (`Al-Ghanī`) and unique oneness (`Tawḥīd`). This rejection is not merely a denial of physical procreation but a comprehensive theological stance against any form of association that compromises God's transcendent nature.

Example

The Qur'an repeatedly and emphatically denies the attribution of a son to God. For instance, in Surah Maryam, it states:

مَا كَانَ لِلَّهِ أَن يَتَّخِذَ مِن وَلَدٍ ۖ سُبْحَانَهُ ۚ إِذَا قَضَىٰ أَمْرًا فَإِنَّمَا يَقُولُ لَهُ كُن فَيَكُونُ

Mā kāna lillāhi an yattakhidha min waladin ۖ subḥānahu ۚ idhā qaḍā amran fa-innamā yaqūlu lahu kun fa-yakūn

"It is not befitting for Allah to take a son. Exalted is He! When He decrees a matter, He only says to it, 'Be,' and it is."

- Qur'an 19:35

This denial is further reinforced by narrations from the Ahl al-Bayt. Imam al-Baqir (a), commenting on Qur'an 17:111 (`وقل الحمد لله الذي لم يتخذ ولدا ولم يكن له شريك في الملك ولم يكن له ولي من الذل وكبره تكبيرا`), clarifies the meaning of God having no `walī min al-dhull` (guardian from humiliation):

لم يذل فيحتاج إلى ولي فينصره.

Lam yadhillu fa-yaḥtāju ilā walīyin fa-yanṣuruhu.

"He was not humiliated so as to need a guardian to help Him."

- Tafsīr al-Qummī, Vol. 2, p. 30

This profound theological point is also captured in a supplication taught by Imam al-Sadiq (a):

والحمد لله الذي لم يتخذ ولدا ولم يكن له شريك في الملك ولم يكن له ولى من الذل وكبره تكبيرا

Wal-ḥamdu lillāhi alladhī lam yattakhidh waladan wa lam yakun lahu sharīkun fī al-mulk wa lam yakun lahu walīyun min al-dhull wa kabbirhu takbīrā

"And praise be to Allah, who has not taken a son, and has no partner in [His] dominion, nor any protector out of weakness; and magnify Him with [all] magnificence."

- Tafsīr al-ʿAyyāshī, Vol. 2, p. 320

Imam al-Sadiq (a) further articulates the implications of denying sonship:

الحمد لله الذي لم يلد فيورث ولم يولد فيشارك.

Al-ḥamdu lillāhi alladhī lam yalid fa-yūrath wa lam yūlad fa-yushārak.

"Praise be to Allah who neither begets, so that He may inherit, nor is begotten, so that He may have a partner."

- Al-Tawḥīd (from Biḥār al-Anwār, Vol. 3, p. 256)

The Prophet Muhammad (s) himself, when faced with claims of sonship, responded with a strong affirmation of God's transcendence, as interpreted by Ali ibn Ibrahim (Qummi) regarding Qur'an 43:81 (`قل إن كان للرحمن ولد فأنا أول العابدين`):

يعني أول الآنفين له أن يكون له ولد.

Yaʿnī awwalu al-ʾānifīna lahu an yakūna lahu walad.

"Meaning, the first to disdain/reject that He has a son."

- Tafsīr Ali ibn Ibrahim (from Biḥār al-Anwār, Vol. 3, p. 256)

This interpretation also extends to the concept of "suspension of the impossible upon the impossible" (`taʿlīq al-muḥāl bi-al-muḥāl`), meaning that if God *were* to have a son (an impossibility), then the Prophet would be the first to worship such a son, thereby highlighting the absolute impossibility of the premise itself.

Application

These doctrinal proofs, stemming directly from the Qur'an and the teachings of the Ahl al-Bayt (a), unequivocally demonstrate that `ittikhādh walad` is an impossible attribute for God. The denial is rooted in God's **absolute self-sufficiency**, His **lack of need for a partner or heir**, and His **utter transcendence beyond human limitations of procreation or reliance**. The very act of "taking" implies a need, which is antithetical to God's perfection. Therefore, any argument positing divine sonship based on a misinterpretation of `iṣṭifā'` or `ittikhādh` is fundamentally flawed from an Islamic theological standpoint.

Furthermore, as articulated by Shi'i scholars, if the act of "taking a son" were attributed to God, it would imply that created beings could influence or participate in God's dominion, which is also rejected. As Sharīf al-Murtaḍā states:

ولو كان اتّخاذهم الولد فعل اللّه كان قد اتّخذ ولدا، ولو كان قد فعل عباده فعله كان له شريك في الملك، تعالى عن ذلك.

Wa law kāna ittikhādhuhum al-walad fiʿla Allāh kāna qad ittakhadha waladan, wa law kāna qad faʿala ʿibāduhu fiʿlahu kāna lahu sharīkun fī al-mulk, taʿālā ʿan dhālik.

"If their taking of a son were God's act, He would have taken a son, and if His servants' actions were His actions, He would have a partner in dominion, exalted is He above that."

- Tafsīr al-Sharīf al-Murtaḍā (from Biḥār al-Anwār, Vol. 9, p. 145)

This highlights the doctrinal impossibility of `ittikhādh walad` from the perspective of God's **absolute sovereignty and lack of partners**.

Conclusion

The concept of God "taking a son" is unequivocally rejected in Islamic doctrine due to its implications of divine need, partnership, and limitation. The Qur'an and the teachings of the Ahl al-Bayt (a) firmly establish God's **absolute self-sufficiency and unique oneness**, rendering any form of `ittikhādh walad` as a theological impossibility and a grave deviation from pure monotheism (`Tawḥīd`).

3. The Logical Proof

Beyond the textual evidence, the polemical argument is built upon several fundamental logical fallacies. Identifying these errors reveals that the conclusion is not supported by a sound reasoning process. This section details these critical flaws, using both Western and Islamic logical taxonomies.

I. Fallacies of Ambiguity & Presumption

Equivocation
Introduction

The fallacy of equivocation occurs when a key term is used with two or more different meanings within the same argument, leading to a misleading conclusion. The argument appears sound because the word is the same, but the underlying concepts are different.

Example

Consider the word "bank." If someone argues, "I deposited money in the bank, and the bank is next to the river, therefore I deposited money in the river," they are equivocating on the word "bank" (financial institution vs. river bank).

In the polemical argument, the word `iṣṭifā` (to choose/select) is used in two distinct contexts:

  • Usage in Qur'an 39:4: `iṣṭifā` is used hypothetically within a counterfactual statement: لَاصْطَفَىٰ مِمَّا يَخْلُقُ مَا يَشَاءُ

    • laṣṭafā mimmā yakhluqu mā yashāʾu

    ("He could have chosen from what He creates whatever He wills"). Here, it's part of a rhetorical device to negate the idea of "taking a son" by showing that even if God *were* to choose, it would be from creation, not as a son.

  • Usage in Qur'an 3:42: `iṣṭifā` is used factually to describe the divine honoring of Maryam: إِنَّ اللَّهَ اصْطَفَاكِ

    • Inna Allāha iṣṭafāki

    ("Indeed, Allah has chosen you"). This refers to her selection for a specific, noble task: to be the mother of a prophet through a miracle.

Application

The argument commits equivocation by treating these two distinct uses of `iṣṭifā'` as if they were identical in meaning and implication. It falsely equates a hypothetical "choosing from creation" (in a verse denying sonship) with an actual "choosing a servant for a miracle." This creates an illusory connection between a verse of negation (39:4) and a verse of affirmation (3:42), forming a bridge where no logical or linguistic bridge exists.

Conclusion

By failing to distinguish between the hypothetical, negating use of `iṣṭifā'` in 39:4 and its factual, honoring use in 3:42, the argument falls prey to the fallacy of equivocation, rendering its conclusion invalid.

Category Error
Introduction

Ascribing a property to something that could not possibly have that property.

Example

Saying that the number 7 is "angry" or that a triangle is "honest." These are properties that do not apply to the categories of numbers or shapes.

Application

The argument applies the category of "needing or having a son" (an attribute of created, mortal beings) to the category of "The Creator," who is by definition Self-Sufficient, Eternal, and without need. It is a fundamental error to ascribe creaturely attributes to God.

Conclusion

The very premise of the argument commits a category error, making its theological reasoning invalid from the start.

II. Fallacies of Relevance & Formal Logic

Straw Man
Introduction

Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack.

Example

Person A: "I think we should spend less on the military." Person B: "So you want to leave the country defenseless?"

Application

The argument misrepresents the Qur'an's position by treating verse 39:4 (a rhetorical refutation) as a literal blueprint. This creates a weak, distorted version of the Qur'anic argument (a "straw man") that is then "refuted" by a misreading of 3:42. The actual Qur'anic argument, a categorical denial of sonship, is ignored.

Conclusion

The argument does not engage with the actual Qur'anic text but with a distorted version of it, making its refutation irrelevant.

Affirming the Consequent
Introduction

A formal fallacy with the structure: If P, then Q. Q. Therefore, P.

Example

"If it is raining (P), the ground is wet (Q). The ground is wet (Q). Therefore, it is raining (P)." (This is invalid; the ground could be wet for other reasons, like a sprinkler).

Application

The argument claims: "If God wanted a son (P), He would choose (Q). God did choose Maryam (Q). Therefore, God wanted a son (P)." This is a textbook example of this fallacy and is logically invalid.

Conclusion

The argument's structure is formally invalid, meaning its conclusion cannot be true even if its premises were accepted.

III. Fallacies in the Islamic Logical Tradition (`al-Manṭiq`)

Qiyās ma‘a al-Fāriq (Analogy with a Critical Difference)
Introduction

An invalid analogy that ignores a critical difference between the two things being compared.

Example

"An employee should be able to consult a textbook during an exam, because a surgeon gets to use X-rays during surgery." (The analogy fails because the situations and purposes are critically different).

Application

The argument creates a faulty analogy between God "choosing" and human "begetting." The Qur'an itself breaks this by presenting a stronger analogy: Jesus is like Adam (3:59). The critical difference is that both are *creations*, refuting any notion of shared divinity.

Conclusion

The polemical analogy is weak and contradicted by a more powerful, textually-grounded analogy within the Qur'an itself.

Tajrīd al-Naql ‘an Siyāqih (Decontextualization of Revelation)
Introduction

The error of stripping a revealed text from its linguistic, rhetorical, and historical context to force a desired meaning.

Example

Taking the phrase "an eye for an eye" out of its legal context to justify personal revenge.

Application

This is the overarching error of the entire argument. It strips 39:4 of its rhetorical context (the use of `law`) and 3:42 of its doctrinal context (honoring Maryam for a miracle), then decontextualizes both to create an artificial link.

Conclusion

The argument is only possible through a severe decontextualization of the Qur'anic verses, ignoring their clear purpose as established by linguistics, doctrine, and history.

4. The Historical Proof

The consistent understanding of these verses is not a modern development. It is rooted in the earliest history of the Islamic community and the authoritative methodology of the Ahl al-Bayt (a) in resolving theological questions.

The Encounter with the Christians of Najran

Introduction

The most significant historical event clarifying the Islamic position on Jesus's nature is the Prophet Muhammad's (s) debate with the Christian delegation from Najran, which culminated in the challenge of *Mubāhalah* (mutual imprecation). This event, recorded in numerous historical and exegetical sources, provides a foundational understanding of the Islamic stance on Jesus's status as a created servant, not a divine son.

Example

During the encounter, the Christians questioned the Prophet's assertion about Jesus. A key exchange is recorded:

فقالوا له: إلى ما تدعونا؟ فقال: إلى شهادة أن لا إله إلا الله، وأني رسول الله، وأن عيسى عبد مخلوق، يأكل ويشرب ويحدث. قالوا: فمن أبوه؟... فأنزل الله: (إِنَّ مَثَلَ عِيسى عِنْدَ اللَّه كَمَثَلِ آدَمَ)

Fa-qālū lahu: ʾilā mā tadʿūnā? Fa-qāla: ʾilā shahādati ʾan lā ʾilāha ʾillā Allāh, wa ʾannī rasūlu Allāh, wa ʾanna ʿĪsā ʿabdun makhlūq, yaʾkulu wa yashrabu wa yuḥdithu. Qālū: Fa-man ʾabūhu?... Fa-anzala Allāhu: (ʾinna mathala ʿĪsā ʿinda Allāhi ka-mathali Ādama)

"They said to him: 'To what do you call us?' He said: 'To the testimony that there is no god but Allah, that I am the Messenger of Allah, and that Jesus is a created servant, who eats, drinks, and has bodily needs.' They said: 'Then who is his father?'... Then Allah revealed: **'Indeed, the example of Jesus to Allah is like that of Adam...'**"

- Al-Burhān fī Tafsīr al-Qur’ān (from Tafsīr al-Qummī)

This exchange clearly demonstrates the core of the theological disagreement and the Qur'anic response.

Application

The Prophet's immediate response was to affirm Jesus's status as a "created servant" (`ʿabd makhlūq`) and to use the analogy of Adam (Qur'an 3:59) to refute the argument from fatherlessness. This historical context highlights that the Qur'anic verses were revealed specifically to counter the notion of divine sonship, not to provide a pathway for it. When the debate reached an impasse, the Prophet proposed *Mubāhalah*. The Christian leaders' private conversation before accepting the challenge revealed their understanding of the Prophet's spiritual authority and the potential consequences:

إن باهلنا بقومه باهلناه، فإنه ليس بنبي، وإن باهلنا بأهل بيته خاصة فلا نباهله فإنه لا يقدم على أهل بيته إلا وهو صادق.

In bāhalanā bi-qawmihi bāhalnāhu, fa-ʾinnahu laysa bi-nabī, wa ʾin bāhalanā bi-ʾahli baytihi khāṣṣatan fa-lā nubāhiluhu fa-ʾinnahu lā yuqaddimu ʿalā ʾahli baytihi ʾillā wa huwa ṣādiq.

"'If he challenges us... with his people, we will accept... But if he challenges us with his immediate family (`ahl baytihi`) only, we will not, for he would not bring forth his family unless he is truthful.'"

- Al-Burhān fī Tafsīr al-Qur’ān

Their subsequent refusal to face the Prophet and his Ahl al-Bayt (a) in the Mubahala was a historical concession to the truth of the Prophet's claim and the unique spiritual station of his family, further solidifying the Islamic position on Jesus's created nature.

Conclusion

The historical record of the Najran encounter unequivocally demonstrates that the Islamic position, as articulated by the Prophet (s) and his Ahl al-Bayt (a), was always a direct refutation of divine sonship. This historical context reinforces the linguistic and doctrinal proofs, showing a consistent and unchanging understanding of these Qur'anic verses from the very inception of Islam.

Final Verdict

Lady Maryam (a) was chosen not to bear a son *for* God, but to bear a Sign *from* God. She is the mother of God's mighty Prophet and His Word (`kalimah`), but she is the mother of a created servant, not a divine offspring. Her selection elevates her as a paragon of purity and submission, a witness to God's absolute power and indivisible Oneness (`Tawḥīd`).
