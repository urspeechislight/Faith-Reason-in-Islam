A Theological & Linguistic Analysis

Examining Divine Selection and Sonship in Qur'an 3:42 and 39:4

The Core Argument at a Glance

This analysis examines the argument that links two Qur'anic verses to support the concept of divine sonship. From a Shi'i perspective, this linkage is untenable. This application breaks down the position into three key areas: Linguistic, Doctrinal, and Logical. The table below summarizes the core of the analysis.

Verse in Question Polemical Interpretation Shi'i Analytical Position
Qur'an 39:4
"Had Allah willed to take a son..."		 Presents a **conditional blueprint** for how God could take a son. Presents a **hypothetical refutation**. The word `لَوْ` (law/had) introduces an impossible scenario to demonstrate its absurdity. Its purpose is **negation**.
Qur'an 3:42
"Indeed, Allah has chosen you."		 Maryam's selection is the **fulfillment** of the condition in 39:4. Maryam's selection was to honor her purity and designate her for a **miracle** (the virgin birth), not to bear a divine son.
The Logical Link The two verses connect to prove Jesus is the chosen, divine son. **No link exists.** Connecting a hypothetical verse of negation with a factual verse of affirmation is a severe logical error.

1. The Linguistic Proof

The argument conflates distinct Arabic concepts. Classical linguistics and the Qur'an's own vocabulary demonstrate that "choosing a servant" and "taking a son" are separate ideas. This section explores the direct evidence for this distinction, focusing on the key vocabulary.

The Function of `Law` (لَوْ): The Particle of Impossibility

The entire premise of Qur'an 39:4 hinges on the word `law` (if/had). In Arabic grammar, `law` is not a simple conditional. It introduces a counterfactual, a scenario that is impossible or contrary to reality. It is used for the sake of argument, not to propose a real possibility.

لَوْ: حَرْفٌ يَقْتَضِي امْتِناعَ ما يَلِيهِ واسْتِلْزامَهُ لِتالِيهِ

"`Law`: A particle that necessitates the impossibility of what follows it, and its necessary connection to what comes after."

- Lisān al-`Arab

This means the clause "Allah willed to take a son" is presented as an impossible premise. The verse's function is to say, "Even if we were to entertain this absurd and impossible idea, here is how flawed it would be." Classical poetry consistently uses `law` to express an impossible wish or a lament over something that cannot be, as seen in the verse by the pre-Islamic poet Imru' al-Qais:

أَلا رُبَّ يَومٍ لَكَ مِنهُنَّ صالِحٌ ... وَلا سِيَّما يَومٍ بِدارَةِ جُلجُلِ

فَلَوْ أَنَّهَا نَفْسٌ تَمُوتُ سَوِيَّةً ... وَلَكِنَّهَا نَفْسٌ تَسَاقَطُ أَنْفُسَا

"Many a fine day have I had with them, especially the day at Darat Juljul... If only it were one soul that died at once, but it is a soul from which other souls fall away."

- Mu`allaqa of Imru' al-Qais

Here, the poet uses `law` to wish for an impossible, simpler death, contrasting it with his painful reality. The particle establishes a wish contrary to fact. In the Qur'an, it establishes a premise contrary to divine reality.

Lexical Meanings: `Iṣṭifā'` vs. `Ittikhādh`

Iṣṭifā' (اِصْطِفَاء)

Root: `ص-ف-و` (Purity)

Concept: To Choose / To Select the Purest

This term describes an act of **honoring a creature** based on its inherent qualities. It is a selection from among existing creation for a specific, noble task. The one chosen (`muṣṭafā`) remains a servant of God.

الصَّفْوُ: خلوص الشيء من الكدر... واصطفاه: اختاره.

"Al-Ṣafw: The freedom of a thing from any impurity... and `iṣṭafāhu` means he chose him."

- Lisān al-`Arab

Ittikhādh (اِتِّخَاذ)

Root: `أ-خ-ذ` (To Take)

Concept: To Take / To Adopt / To Designate

This term describes an act of **designation or adoption**. It implies taking something and making it into something for oneself, suggesting a need or desire. This is why the Qur'an uses it specifically for the prohibited act of "taking a son."

واتَّخَذْتُه وليّاً أَي صَيَّرْتُه. وقوله تعالى: واتَّخَذَ الله إِبراهيمَ خَلِيلاً؛ أَي صَيَّرَه خَلِيلاً

"And `ittakhadhtuhu waliyyan` means I made him a friend/ally. And the statement of the Exalted: `And Allah took (`ittakhadha`) Ibrāhīm as an intimate friend (`khalīl`)`; meaning, He made him an intimate friend."

- Lisān al-`Arab

2. The Doctrinal Proof

Shi'i exegesis (`tafsīr`) is built upon the teachings of the Ahl al-Bayt (a). Their narrations clarify the explicit purpose of these verses and preemptively refute objections by explaining the true status of Jesus (`Īsā) and his mother Maryam.

Evidence 1: Qur'an 39:4 is a Verse of Invalidation

*Tafsīr al-Qummī* explicitly states that Qur'an 39:4 was revealed as a direct rebuttal to those who attribute a son to God.

هو ما قالت النصارى ان المسيح ابن الله... فقال الله عز وجل إبطالا له... وجواب هؤلاء الذين زعموا ذلك في سورة الزمر في قوله : لو أراد الله ان يتخذ ولدا لاصطفى مما يخلق ما يشاء سبحانه.

"This is what the Christians said, that the Messiah is the son of God... So Allah... said, in **invalidation (`ibṭālan`)** of it... And the **answer (`jawāb`)** to those who made this claim is in Sūrat al-Zumar, in His statement: **'Had Allah willed to take a son, He could have chosen from what He creates whatever He wills. Glory be to Him!'**"

- Tafsīr al-Qummī, Vol. 2, p. 69

Key Takeaway: The Imams taught that this verse is the divine "answer" and "invalidation." Using it to support the claim is a direct reversal of its revealed purpose.

Evidence 2: The Meaning of Jesus's Unique Titles

Polemics often point to titles like "Word from Him" (Qur'an 3:45) and "Spirit from Him" (4:171) as proof of divinity. The Imams clarified these terms to preempt this error.

  • A "Word" (`Kalimah`): This signifies the creative command of God, "Be!" (`kun`), which brought Jesus into existence without a father. It does not imply he is an uncreated part of God. The Qur'an states about Maryam: "She believed in the Words of her Lord" (66:12), using the plural (`kalimāt`) for divine commands.
  • A "Spirit" (`Rūḥ`): This refers to the life-giving spirit created by God and sent to Maryam. It is a noble creation from God, not a part of God's essence. The same term is used for the creation of Adam: "I breathed into him of My Spirit" (15:29).

Key Takeaway: These titles signify the miraculous nature of Jesus's creation, not his divinity. They highlight his status as a special creation, a sign (`āyah`), and a servant of God, as affirmed in Qur'an 4:172: "The Messiah would never be too proud to be a servant of Allah."

Evidence 3: The Purpose of the Virgin Birth

The Qur'an itself explains the purpose of the virgin birth. It was not to produce a divine son, but to manifest God's absolute power and create a sign for humanity.

وَلِنَجْعَلَهُ آيَةً لِلنَّاسِ وَرَحْمَةً مِنَّا

"...And that We may make him a sign (`āyah`) for the people and a mercy from Us."

- Qur'an 19:21

To shut down any claim that this sign implies divinity, the Qur'an immediately compares Jesus to Adam: "Indeed, the example of Jesus to Allah is like that of Adam. He created him from dust; then He said to him, 'Be,' and he was" (3:59). If being created without a father made Jesus divine, then Adam, created without a father or a mother, would be even more so, a conclusion no one accepts.

Key Takeaway: The virgin birth is a demonstration of God's creative will, placing Jesus in the line of prophets, not in the realm of divinity.

3. The Logical Proof

Beyond the textual evidence, the argument is built upon several fundamental logical fallacies. Identifying these errors reveals that the conclusion is not supported by a sound reasoning process. This section details these critical flaws.

Flaw 1: The Fallacy of Equivocation

This fallacy occurs when a single word is used with two different meanings within the same argument, creating an illusion of connection.

  • Usage in 39:4: The word `iṣṭafā` (to choose) is used hypothetically as part of a rhetorical device to negate the idea of "taking a son." It describes a selection *from creation*.
  • Usage in 3:42: The word `iṣṭafā` is used factually to describe the divine honoring of Maryam for a specific task: to be the mother of a prophet.
  • The Error: The argument treats these two distinct uses as if they were identical. It falsely equates "hypothetically choosing a creature to prove a point" with "actually choosing a servant for a miracle." This creates a bridge where none exists.

Flaw 2: The Category Error & Straw Man Argument

This argument commits two related fallacies by misrepresenting the Islamic concept of God.

Category Error: The concept of "taking a son" (`ittikhādh walad`) belongs exclusively to the category of created beings. It implies needs like companionship, succession, or physical procreation. God, by definition, is The Self-Sufficient (`Al-Ghanī`) and Transcendent. To suggest God would "take a son" is to incorrectly apply a property of the created to the Creator.

Straw Man: The argument attacks a crude, physical idea of "sonship" that Islam has never proposed for God. The Islamic principle of `Tawḥīd` rejects *any* form of partnership or progeny, whether physical or spiritual, as it violates God's absolute oneness. The Qur'an is not merely denying a physical son; it is denying any concept that compromises God's unique and indivisible nature.

Flaw 3: Misinterpreting Hypothetical Language

The argument fails to recognize the function of counterfactual conditionals in Arabic rhetoric.

The particle `law` (لَوْ) in Qur'an 39:4 introduces an impossible condition, a statement contrary to fact, used for the sake of argument. It is not a blueprint for a possible future action. It functions to say, "Even if we were to entertain this absurd and impossible premise, here is how flawed it would be." The verse immediately concludes with `subḥānahu` ("Glory be to Him!"), the ultimate expression of divine transcendence that dismisses the entire hypothetical. To treat this as a literal possibility that was later fulfilled is to ignore the verse's clear rhetorical function of negation.

Final Verdict

Lady Maryam (a) was chosen not to bear a son *for* God, but to bear a Sign *from* God. She is the mother of God's mighty Prophet and His Word (`kalimah`), but she is the mother of a created servant, not a divine offspring. Her selection elevates her as a paragon of purity and submission, a witness to God's absolute power and indivisible Oneness (`Tawḥīd`).
